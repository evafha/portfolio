Tangible and Sensorial Interaction Design

Create a board game that promotes social interaction and learning.

About the project

Create a board game with inclusive gameplay mechanics and large, easy-to-handle pieces. It needs to be suitable for families with children of diverse abilities or special education classrooms. The game should also promote social interaction and learning.

Target Group

Families with children of diverse abilities, and special education classrooms.

The Team and My Role

The team consisted of four interacion design students.

Timeline

2 months

Tools

Figma, Adobe Illustrator, Laser Cutter, TinkerCad, Laminating Machine

My Design Process

Innsight

Interview

We conducted semi-structured interviews to gather qualitative insights on the topic. The interviews were divided into two main groups: professionals and users.

After conducting interviews with professionals, it became clear that children have individual differences, abilities and skill levels. For example, some children may exhibit disruptive behavior due to difficulty expressing emotions, struggle with concentration in noisy environments, or prefer playing alone or with few people. Professionals emphasized that concentration difficulties are common among children.

Based on this user insight, we focused on children aged 5-12 with developmental disorders. By narrowing our target group, we could better tailor the board game to meet their specific needs.

Personas

To ensure a good representation of our user groups, we developed three personas:

  • Child: Reflects how children engage with board games, focusing on designing games that are both entertaining and supportive of various challenges.
  • Family Member: Focuses on family-friendly games that encourage both cooperation and friendly competition, while considering reduced color vision.
  • Professional: Emphasizes how games can be used to promote learning, develop social skills, and support educational methods in the classroom, engaging students and promoting critical thinking.

Markus is an energetic and curious boy with ADHD who enjoys interactive and visual games. His father, Lars, has reduced color vision and prefers games with clear symbols so he can fully participate. Marit, a special education teacher, uses similar games in her classroom to promote inclusion and collaboration. The board game is designed to meet various needs, with a focus on Markus as the central figure during many of our phases.

Ideate Ideas and Concepts

We used various methods, including Crazy 8 and creating a mood board, to generate a range of ideas. We narrowed it down to three concepts and used a decision matrix to determine which one scored the highest. The final result included elements from all the concepts.

Sketches

Prototyping

For the prototyping phase of our board game, we began by creating detailed drawings to outline the structure and visual elements. These drawings allowed us to get a better sense of how the pieces would interact with each other. To bring our ideas to life, we then crafted a physical mockup using colored paper, which allowed us to experiment more with the layout and shape of the board, as well as the height.

We laser cut all the pieces, including the board, the "smaragds" and the different shapes for the game. Each piece was designed to make the game visually appealing and easy to use. After cutting the pieces, we painted each one to add a personal touch.

Final Product

Introducing "SMARAGD" an exciting board game where the goal is to collect the most emeralds in your "piggy bank". Players match patterns with point cards to win emeralds.

  • Number of players: 1-8
  • Game duration: 2 minutes per round, with the option to play with or without time pressure
  • Number of rounds: 3 rounds, or as many as desired

The player who has collected the most emeralds after all rounds is crowned the winner.

"SMARAGD" offers challenging and engaging entertainment for both children and adults, and can be adapted to suit different player preferences and needs.

Testing

The game was generally well-received, and the children found it fun and engaging. They enjoyed the colors, characters, and the excitement of competing and counting emeralds after each round. Some had difficulty understanding the rules at first, but images and examples helped.

A few children were frustrated by resetting pieces after mistakes, or by not finishing their scorecards in time. However, they quickly learned the rules and improved with each round. The pedagogue involved, appreciated the game's flexibility, noting that it allowed for different ways to play.

The outcomes varied across age groups. The youngest group (5-6 years old) often needed guidance and confirmation that their patterns were correct, as they frequently made small mistakes or missed a piece. Some also needed reminders to place the correct number of emeralds and draw new cards.

The older group (7-10 years old) mostly managed on their own, placing emeralds, drawing new cards, and keeping track of their pieces. Despite minor errors and challenges, the children expressed a strong desire to continue playing.

What have I learned from this project

In this project, I have truly understood the value of regular feedback and adjustments throughout the process. Breaking the project down into different phases has greatly helped in keeping track and structuring our work. Additionally, I have gained insight into how challenging it can be to design something for a target group that we did not have direct access to. The experience and process of breaking down a problem, developing a design, and creating a testable prototype have been very educational. It is challenging to create a product from scratch and ensure all the components align with each other. Furthermore, considering universal design has been both exciting and rewarding, offering valuable experience in addressing physical, tactile, and sensory challenges.

Key Insights

A key takeaway is the importance of involving users and professionals earlier to identify and address issues more effectively. While working on this project provided valuable insights into designing without direct access to the target group, I look forward to future projects with more opportunities for user feedback. These experiences have given me valuable insights that I will carry forward.
